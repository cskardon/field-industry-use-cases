= Track and Trace

== Introduction

Supply chain track and trace in life sciences is the ability to follow the movement of pharmaceutical products, medical devices, or ingredients through the supply chain from manufacture to the point of dispensing or use. Regulatory requirements such as the (https://www.fda.gov/drugs/drug-supply-chain-integrity/drug-supply-chain-security-act-dscsa[Drug Supply Chain Security Act (DSCSA)]) in the United States and the (https://ec.europa.eu/health/medicinal-products/eudralex/eudralex-volume-4_en[Falsified Medicines Directive]) in the European Union mandate serialization and traceability to combat counterfeiting, diversion, and recalls. Businesses need to answer questions such as: Where did this batch go? Which lots are affected by a recall? Who handled this serialized unit? Graph databases excel at representing and querying these multi-hop custody and movement relationships across manufacturers, distributors, wholesalers, and dispensers.

In the food industry, traceability and recall requirements are governed by regulations such as the U.S. Food Safety Modernization Act (FSMA) and the EU General Food Law (https://www.legislation.gov.uk/eur/2002/178/contents[Regulation (EC) No 178/2002]). Food producers and distributors must be able to identify and trace affected lots and typically respond to regulatory trace requests within 24 hours, ensuring rapid containment of contaminated or unsafe products.

=== Challenges

Traditional ERP-based reporting approaches are fundamentally ill-suited for this challenge. Tracing a single faulty batch through a complex, multi-level ingredient and production hierarchy can take hours or even days when relying on relational database joins across movement tables. The deeper the production hierarchy, the worse the performance degrades.

== Scenario

Imagine a pharmaceutical manufacturer producing tablet batches, each derived from various raw materials and packaging components (API, excipients, foil, cartons), and processed through a multi-step manufacturing pipeline. These intermediate and finished batches are then combined into deliveries distributed to customers and wholesalers. One day, the quality team receives an alert that an input API batch is potentially contaminated. The organization must instantly identify not only which finished deliveries could contain traces of the suspect lot, but also the chain of intermediate process orders and batches through which contamination may have propagated. At the same time, a reverse trace is needed: for any particular customer delivery, the full lineage of all raw material batches, transfer events, and plant movements must be reconstructed.

The complexity arises because each batch can participate in many-to-many relationships: a single raw batch may feed multiple process orders and spawn several derived batches, which are then packaged together in varied configurations. Each delivery could aggregate material from multiple sources, and one suspect input could affect dozens of downstream customers across numerous supply chain handoffs. Matching and traversing these movement, transformation, and delivery relationships is a classic graph problem—one where identifying all connected paths, impacts, and upstream/downstream nodes across variable depths is virtually impossible (or prohibitively slow) using traditional relational queries but is native, fast, and flexible in a graph database.

== Solution

=== Why Graph?

The traceability problem is inherently a graph traversal problem. The pharmaceutical ingredient supply chain is a directed graph of batches connected through production and logistics events. Neo4j's native graph storage and Cypher path traversal engine make multi-hop queries across this structure orders of magnitude faster than equivalent relational approaches.
Key advantages of the graph approach are the following.
Index-free adjacency. Each node directly references its neighbors, so traversal cost scales with the number of relevant paths rather than the size of the full dataset.
Variable-depth path queries. **Cypher's variable-length path syntax natively handles unlimited-depth traversal with a single, readable query.**
Rich relationship types. Movement semantics such as issued, received, transferred, and delivered are encoded directly as typed, directional relationships with quantitative properties.
Near real-time sync. Delta loading via Cypher scripts every 5 minutes keeps the graph current with ERP state, ensuring no material movement is missed in a recall scope calculation.

This approach meets EU FMD reporting requirements within the 24-hour detection window and is equally capable of supporting DSCSA electronic tracing obligations in the US market, transforming a painful multi-hour ERP reporting exercise into a seconds-long graph traversal.

This implementation enabled traversing 50,000+ deliveries across up to 52 supply chain levels in just ~2 seconds, providing near real-time trace results at scale.


=== Architecture
The production architecture consists of four layers.

==== ERP Replication Layer
The SAP ERP system replicates movement data in real time to a Microsoft SQL Server instance hosted on Azure using SAP SLT (System Landscape Transformation). This staging layer buffers raw movement records before they are loaded into Neo4j.

==== Graph Loading Layer
Cypher scripts run on a 5-minute delta cycle, reading new movement records from SQL Server and loading them into the Neo4j Traceability Graph. During loading, aggregated TOTAL_* relationships are calculated and written to the graph alongside the raw movement data.

==== Traceability Graph (Neo4j)
The core analytical engine. A path calculation engine implemented in Cypher handles forward and backward traversals. All traceability queries and reports execute against this graph.

==== Frontend and Monitoring
A web-based traceability dashboard queries Neo4j via the Neo4j driver, presenting batch-to-delivery reports, affected customer lists, and mass balance views. Query performance and system health are monitored via Prometheus, with logs forwarded to Elasticsearch and Kibana.

== Modelling

The data model for pharmaceutical supply chain track-and-trace is designed around a few core node entities and the movement relationships that connect them through the manufacturing and distribution process. This structure enables powerful lineage, path, and impact queries across all levels of the supply network.

**Core Node Labels**

- `Batch`: Represents a uniquely identified lot of material (API, excipient, intermediate, or finished product). Holds properties such as batch number, material code, plant, manufacture/expiry dates, and status.
- `ProcessOrder`: A manufacturing or packaging operation that consumes input batches and produces an output batch (i.e., transformation processes like synthesis, formulation, or packaging steps).
- `PurchaseOrder`: Captures inbound procurement events (raw materials or APIs received from vendors), associated with one or more created batches.
- `DeliveryLine`: A shipment event tying a specific batch (and quantity) to a customer order.
- `Delivery`: The overall customer shipment (header), which may contain one or many `DeliveryLine` items, along with destination, date, and regulatory trace info.

**Core Relationships (Lineage and Movement)**

- `PURCHASE_RECEIVED`: (:PurchaseOrder)-[:PURCHASE_RECEIVED]->(:Batch)  
  A material batch is received from an external supplier against a purchase order.

- `BATCH_ISSUED`: (:Batch)-[:BATCH_ISSUED]->(:ProcessOrder)
  A batch is issued/consumed as input for a specific process order.

- `BATCH_RECEIVED`: (:ProcessOrder)-[:BATCH_RECEIVED]->(:Batch)
  Completion of a process order produces a new batch output.

- `BATCH_TRANSFER`: (:Batch)-[:BATCH_TRANSFER]->(:Batch)
  Represents a transfer event within the same plant or warehouse between batches (e.g., for packaging or re-testing).

- `PLANT_TRANSFER`: (:Batch)-[:PLANT_TRANSFER]->(:Batch)
  Batch is transferred between sites (moves from one plant/location to another).

- `DELIVERY_RECEIVED`: (:DeliveryLine)-[:DELIVERY_RECEIVED]->(:Batch)
  A shipment event that logs receipt of a batch as part of a delivery document.

- `MAPPED_DELIVERY`: (:DeliveryLine)-[:MAPPED_DELIVERY]->(:DeliveryLine)
  For cases where inbound and outbound deliveries are cross-referenced (e.g., returns, recalls, re-shipments).

- `TOTAL_*` relationships represent pre-aggregated summary movements between nodes for analytics and fast scope calculation (e.g., `:TOTAL_ISSUED`, `:TOTAL_RECEIVED`)
  

**Example Cypher: Creating a basic lineage fragment**

Suppose we receive a new batch of API via purchase order, which is then issued to a process order and results in a finished batch. Here's how this might look in Cypher:

```cypher
CREATE (apiPo:PurchaseOrder {number: 'PO123', supplier: 'Acme Chemicals'})
CREATE (apiBatch:Batch {batchNo: 'B001', material: 'API-XYZ', plant: 'PlantA'})
CREATE (formulation:ProcessOrder {orderNo: 'PR456', type: 'Formulation'})
CREATE (finalBatch:Batch {batchNo: 'B002', material: 'Tablet-XYZ', plant: 'PlantA'})
CREATE (deliveryLine:DeliveryLine {lineNo: 'DL789', quantity: 1000})
CREATE (delivery:Delivery {deliveryNo: 'D001', customer: 'PharmaDistCo'})

MERGE (apiPo)-[:PURCHASE_RECEIVED]->(apiBatch)
MERGE (apiBatch)-[:BATCH_ISSUED]->(formulation)
MERGE (formulation)-[:BATCH_RECEIVED]->(finalBatch)
MERGE (finalBatch)-[:DELIVERY_RECEIVED]->(deliveryLine)
MERGE (deliveryLine)-[:IN_DELIVERY]->(delivery)
```

This produces a clear lineage:

`PurchaseOrder` → `[PURCHASE_RECEIVED]` → `Batch` → `[BATCH_ISSUED]` → `ProcessOrder` → `[BATCH_RECEIVED]` → `Batch` → `[DELIVERY_RECEIVED]` → `DeliveryLine` → `[IN_DELIVERY]` → `Delivery`

Such a chain allows immediate backward and forward tracing of materials through all manufacturing and shipment events, enabling high-confidence scope calculation in recalls and audit readiness.

For large-scale supply chains, this model supports multi-hop traversals, "find all affected customers for a given (recalled) batch", and detailed movement analysis directly via Cypher queries.

=== Data Model - Aggregated TOTAL Relationships

Because traceability only requires traversal once between any two points, raw movements are aggregated into a single TOTAL_* relationship per source-target pair. These relationships carry calculated properties including the percentage of the issued material present in the received batch (partInReceived), which enables quantitative contamination scope and mass balance analysis.

== Cypher Queries

Example queries:

* *Path of a serialized unit*: Start from a `SerializedUnit` by `serial_number`, traverse `SHIPPED_IN` to `Shipment`, then `FROM`/`TO` to `Location`, and optionally `OPERATED_BY` to `Party`, ordered by time.
* *All locations that received a batch*: Start from `Batch` by `batch_number`, traverse to `Shipment` and then `TO`->`Location`, return distinct locations (and optionally parties).
* *Full chain of custody for a unit*: Same path as the first query, returning a chronological list of locations and parties.

One important design decision to note: `partInReceived = 1.0` is set on all `TOTAL_RECEIVED` relationships. This is necessary because the forward trace in Query 1 uses an unfiltered `reduce()` that would propagate `null` if any relationship in the path lacks the property. The packaging steps (blistering, cartoning) also use `partInReceived = 1.0` for the tablet batch since contamination is tracked as a drug content fraction, not a mass fraction. The packaging material relationships carry `0.0` to reflect that foil and cartons contribute no suspect API.

=== Forward Trace: Find All Deliveries Impacted by a Faulty Batch

This is the core contamination recall query. Starting from a known suspect batch, it traverses the entire downstream graph through process orders, derived batches, plant transfers, and deliveries up to an arbitrary depth.
[source, cypher, role=noheader]
----
// Forward trace: find all deliveries downstream of a suspect batch
WITH '1100_1101475_2899901' AS batchNr
MATCH path = (startBatch:Batch {batchNr: batchNr})
  -[:TOTAL_ISSUED | TOTAL_RECEIVED | TOTAL_BATCH_TRANSFER |
     TOTAL_PLANT_TRANSFER | TOTAL_MAPPED_DELIVERY |
     TOTAL_ISSUE_DELIVERY*1..100]->
  (delivery:Delivery)
WITH delivery,
     length(path) AS pathLength,
     [rel IN relationships(path) | rel.partInReceived] AS percBatchUsed
RETURN
  delivery.deliveryNr           AS deliveryNr,
  delivery.soldToCustomer       AS soldToCustomer,
  delivery.shipToCustomer       AS shipToCustomer,
  delivery.lastPostingDate      AS lastPostingDate,
  pathLength,
  reduce(perc = 1.0, p IN percBatchUsed | perc * p) AS percBatchUsedInDelivery

ORDER BY pathLength ASC
----

=== Backward Trace: Find All Raw Materials in a Delivery

Starting from a specific delivery, this query traverses backward to identify every raw material or API batch and its contributing supplier batch that was used in the product.

[source, cypher, role=noheader]
----
// Backward trace: identify all raw material inputs for a given delivery
WITH 'DEL-2024-001' AS deliveryNr
MATCH path = (delivery:Delivery {deliveryNr: $deliveryNr})

      TOTAL_PLANT_TRANSFER | TOTAL_MAPPED_DELIVERY |
      TOTAL_ISSUE_DELIVERY*1..100]-
  (rawBatch:Batch)

// Leaf nodes with no incoming TOTAL_RECEIVED are raw material batches
WHERE NOT (rawBatch)<-[:TOTAL_RECEIVED]-()

RETURN DISTINCT
  rawBatch.batchNr          AS rawBatchNr,
  rawBatch.materialNr       AS materialNr,
  rawBatch.materialDesc     AS materialDescription,
  rawBatch.plant            AS plant,
  rawBatch.bestBeforeDate   AS bestBeforeDate,
  length(path)              AS depth
ORDER BY depth

=== Aggregating TOTAL_ISSUED Relationships

This query aggregates the granular raw BATCH_ISSUED movements between a batch and a process order into a single TOTAL_ISSUED relationship with quantity and proportion metadata. It runs as part of the 5-minute delta loading cycle.

[source, cypher, role=noheader]
----
// Aggregate raw BATCH_ISSUED movements into TOTAL_ISSUED
MATCH (b:Batch)-[r:BATCH_ISSUED]->(po:ProcessOrder)

WITH b, po,
     sum(r.quantity) AS issuedQty

// Retrieve total received quantity for this process order output
MATCH (po)-[recv:BATCH_RECEIVED]->(outputBatch:Batch)
WITH b, po, outputBatch, issuedQty,
     sum(recv.quantity) AS totalReceived

MERGE (b)-[ti:TOTAL_ISSUED]->(po)
SET ti.totalIssued    = issuedQty,
    ti.partInReceived = CASE
                          WHEN totalReceived > 0
                          THEN issuedQty / totalReceived
                          ELSE 0
                        END

MERGE (po)-[tr:TOTAL_RECEIVED]->(outputBatch)
SET tr.totalReceived = totalReceived

// Query: Retrieve all TOTAL_ISSUED relationships with their quantities, showing which batch was issued to which process order, and the total quantity issued.

// List TOTAL_ISSUED relationships by source batch and target process order
MATCH (b:Batch)-[ti:TOTAL_ISSUED]->(po:ProcessOrder)
RETURN
  b.batchNr            AS batchNumber,
  po.orderNo           AS processOrderNumber,
  ti.totalIssued       AS totalIssuedQuantity,
  ti.partInReceived    AS partInReceived
ORDER BY b.batchNr, po.orderNo
----

=== Regulatory Summary Report for a Batch Recall

Returns the key KPIs required for a regulatory submission under EU FMD or DSCSA: total deliveries affected, unique customers, impacted delivery batches, and maximum traversal depth across the production graph.

[source, cypher, role=noheader]
----
// Regulatory summary report for a faulty batch
WITH '1100_1101475_2899901' AS batchNr
MATCH path = (startBatch:Batch {batchNr: batchNr})
  -[:TOTAL_ISSUED | TOTAL_RECEIVED | TOTAL_BATCH_TRANSFER |
     TOTAL_PLANT_TRANSFER |
     TOTAL_ISSUE_DELIVERY*1..100]->
  (endNode)

WITH endNode, length(path) AS depth,
     labels(endNode) AS nodeLabels

RETURN
  count(CASE WHEN 'Delivery' IN nodeLabels THEN 1 END) AS totalDeliveries,
  count(DISTINCT CASE
    WHEN 'Delivery' IN nodeLabels
    THEN endNode.soldToCustomer END)                        AS uniqueCustomers,
  count(DISTINCT CASE
    WHEN 'Batch' IN nodeLabels
    THEN endNode.batchNr END)                               AS deliveryBatches,
  max(depth)                                               AS maxLevel
----

=== Prioritized Customer Notification List with Contamination Threshold

Returns only customers where the suspect batch constitutes more than a specified percentage of the delivered product. This is useful for risk-tiering recall notifications, for example distinguishing between customers where contamination exceeds 1% versus those with only trace-level exposure.

[source, cypher, role=noheader]
----
// Customers affected above a contamination threshold
WITH '1100_1101475_2899901' AS batchNr,
0.0 as thresholdPercent
MATCH path = (startBatch:Batch {batchNr: batchNr})
  -[:TOTAL_ISSUED | TOTAL_RECEIVED | TOTAL_BATCH_TRANSFER |
     TOTAL_PLANT_TRANSFER | TOTAL_ISSUE_DELIVERY*1..100]->
  (delivery:Delivery)

WITH delivery,thresholdPercent,
     length(path) AS pathLen,
     [rel IN relationships(path) WHERE rel.partInReceived IS NOT NULL
      | rel.partInReceived] AS parts

WITH delivery, pathLen,
     reduce(p = 1.0, x IN parts | p * x) AS percBatchInDelivery

WHERE percBatchInDelivery >= thresholdPercent

RETURN
  delivery.deliveryNr         AS deliveryNr,
  delivery.soldToCustomer     AS customer,
  delivery.shipToCustomer     AS shipTo,
  delivery.lastPostingDate    AS postingDate,
  round(percBatchInDelivery * 100, 4) AS contaminationPercent,
  pathLen                     AS pathLength

ORDER BY contaminationPercent DESC
----

== GDS (Graph Data Science)

Relevant GDS use cases for track-and-trace:

* **Path finding**: Shortest or weighted paths between a source and set of nodes (e.g., from manufacturer to a specific dispenser) for route and delay analysis.
* **Community detection**: Cluster locations or parties by shipment and movement patterns to identify distribution regions or suspicious clusters.
* **Centrality**: Identify highly connected hubs (locations or parties) that are critical for distribution or potential single points of failure.
* **Anomaly detection**: Compare movement patterns of a batch or unit to typical behavior to flag potential diversion or unusual routing.

These algorithms can enhance risk assessment, recall planning, and compliance monitoring on top of the track-and-trace graph.
